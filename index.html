Open like the web.  Learning by everyone. For everyone.

The internet is an amazing place for learning. But recent forays into online learning for higher education replicate a traditional model of campus instruction, instead of embracing the peer-to-peer connected nature of the web.

The opportunity. Peer-to-peer and small pieces loosely joined.

The Internet offers an unprecedented wealth of resources for engaged, interest-driven, lifelong learning. It has long been a haven for self-directed learners who navigate search engines, online forums, wikis, FAQs, and how-to videos to develop expertise and skills. While colleges and universities have had online and distance education for decades, these offerings have, until recently, been largely sequestered from the more informal and demand-driven learning in the wilds of the Internet. In the past few years, however, the movement for putting university offerings online, kicked off by efforts such as Open Course Ware and the Open Educational Resources (OER) movement, has received an infusion of new energy with the advent of massive open online courses (MOOCs) offered by major universities such as Stanford, Harvard, UC Berkeley and MIT.

Not Anti MOOC. But pro open.

The growth of university-sponsored MOOCs has launched a national conversation about innovations in higher education keyed to the digital age. In their current form, however, MOOCs most often replicate the instructional modes of the industrial age: lectures paired with rapid-recall multiple choice questions, and some form of quantitative or qualitative exams, papers, and project assignments. Even as the “massiveness” and “online-ness” may be new, the “course” piece is all too familiar. The large MOOC platforms--Coursera, Udacity and edX--have been criticized for recapitulating the least interesting models of higher education pedagogy with little recognition of the importance of peer learning, the role of user-generated content, and more collaborative learning modes than conventional courses tend to offer. Assessment of learning objectives in MOOCs rarely pays attention to  far too often emphasizes the relatively trivial recapitulation of facts without attention to how students apply theory to practice.
